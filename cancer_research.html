Cancer Vaccine Research with Williams College Professor

Dive into the cutting edge of cancer immunology and vaccine development. This intensive 6-week program is designed for ambitious high school students eager to explore the science behind cancer vaccines, genomic analysis, and the future of personalized medicine. Led by a Williams College biology professor, students will gain hands-on experience in research, data analysis, and ethical considerations shaping the next generation of biomedical breakthroughs.

Taught By

Dr. Damian Turner

Dr. Damian Turner, an immunologist with a PhD from University of Connecticut Health, served as a Biology Professor at Williams College for nine years. There, he taught courses like Introductory Biology and Translational Immunology and led research on respiratory diseases. His work, including a Postdoctoral Fellowship at Columbia University Medical Center focusing on asthma and vaccine development, has resulted in 13 publications. Dr. Turner's research has earned him prestigious awards, highlighting his dedication to advancing immunology and educating future scientists.

Week by Week Curriculum

Week 1: Foundations of Immunology and Cancer Biology

  • Components of the immune system: T cells, B cells, and antigen-presenting cells
  • How CD8 T cells recognize non-self and destroy targets
  • How normal cells become cancerous: mutations, oncogenes, and tumor suppressors
  • Cancer hallmarks: uncontrolled growth, metastasis, and immune evasion

Week 2: Cancer Vaccines: Traditional to Personalized

  • History of cancer vaccines: HPV, hepatitis B, and early therapeutic attempts
  • Why personalization matters: individual genetic differences and unique mutations
  • Different platforms: mRNA, peptides, DNA, and viral vectors

Week 3: Genomic Analysis and Vaccine Design

  • Next-generation sequencing: how we read DNA from tumors
  • Comparing tumor vs. normal DNA to identify cancer-specific mutations
  • Computational algorithms for predicting which mutations make good vaccine targets
  • Discussion of research article assignment

Week 4: Manufacturing and Clinical Translation

  • From computer prediction to physical vaccine: the manufacturing process
  • Understanding clinical trial phases and how new treatments are tested
  • Current personalized vaccine trials and their preliminary results

Week 5: Enhancing Vaccine Efficacy

  • Checkpoint inhibitors: how PD-1/PD-L1 and CTLA-4 inhibitors work
  • Why combining vaccines with checkpoint inhibitors enhances effectiveness
  • Overview of T cell mediated autoimmunity and adverse reactions

Week 6: Future Directions and Ethical Considerations

  • Challenges: cost, accessibility, and manufacturing time
  • Ethical questions in personalized medicine: equity, data privacy, and global access
  • Career pathways in cancer immunotherapy and vaccine development
